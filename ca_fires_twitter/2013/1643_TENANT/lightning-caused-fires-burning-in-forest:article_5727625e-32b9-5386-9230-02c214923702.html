Lightning-caused fires burning in forest

Lightning filled the skies over the Valley and Sierra Nevada Sunday night into Monday morning, with the spectacular show accompanied by loud and rolling, rumbling thunder.

The beautiful display also produced a dozen fires in the Sequoia National Forest, with two of biggest in the Kern River Ranger District.

As of Tuesday afternoon, the Tenant Fire, located near Rancheria Road and Presidio Creek, had burned about 200 acres, and the Shirley Fire between Rancheria Road and Highway 155 near the Alder Creek area had consumed about 500 acres, said Dena Sundberg, information receptionist for the Kern River Ranger District of the Sequoia National Forest.

“We have two air tankers, two helicopters, a Cobra [helicopter] crew, air attack and ground crew,” Sundberg said. “We have approximately 250 personnel on the fires right now.”

Rancheria Road, Oak Flat and Alder Creek campgrounds are all closed to the public.

“They haven’t done any evacuations yet, but they have done reverse 911 calls and voluntary evacuations,” Sundberg said.

Kern County officials announced voluntary evacuation warnings that affected around 400 homes in the Alta Sierra area. Sundberg said the Senior Citizen Center in Lake Isabella has been set up as an evacuation location.

The Shirley blaze is only a mile or two from the communities of Slick Rock and Alta Sierra.

Fire crews were struggling in a dry, rugged area of brush and timber amid continuing lightning strikes, Kern County Fire Engineer Leland Davis said.

“We’re still getting lighting — just all over,” he said.

The fire was one of at least four sparked Sunday night by dry lightning that hit the area around Lake Isabella, Davis said. Another burned about 20 acres.

According to CalFire’s website, there have been over 25,400 lightning strikes in the state since Sunday night.

“We got quite a few down strikes and quite a few fires since midnight [Sunday night],” said Denise Alonzo, acting forest public affairs officer for Giant Sequoia National Monument, Sequoia National Forest.

As of 1 p.m. on Monday, 12 lightning-caused fires have been discovered burning in the Sequoia National Forest and Giant Sequoia National Monument, Alonzo said.

Additional fires discovered on the Kern River Ranger District are Badger, burning near the Tenant Fire, the Cow Fire, burning in Cow Creek, and the Lucas Fire. The Badger, Cow and Lucas fires are all less than one-acre in size.

Two separate storms were the cause of the fires, according to National Weather Service meteorologist Jeff Barlow.

“We had two separate areas of thunderstorms that came through, one at midnight and one at 5:30 a.m. Monday morning.”

Although there was some rain with Sunday night’s storm, according to Barlow there was only 0.10 of an inch of rain. According to local weather observer Greg Chadwell, “there was no measurable rainfall” in Porterville.

According to Alonzo, lightning-caused fires can go undetected for days or weeks until forest materials dry enough to allow flames to spread to the surface.

The near 100-degree temperatures are making it more difficult for firefighters to battle the blazes and the imminent storm moving into the area Monday afternoon with strong winds and more lightning is expected to bring the chance for more fires.

“We are very concerned,” Barlow said. “Especially in the foothills and the Tulare [County] mountains.”

Barlow predicted “continued thunderstorms with gusty winds and hail into [Monday] night,” for the Porterville area, with the storms being much stronger in the foothills and mountains.

Late Monday afternoon, the Weather Service issued a flash flood warning for Tulare County.

Elsewhere, a chain of thunderstorms brought periods of heavy rain and flooding to the high desert. Davis said a mudslide swept a car off a road near Ridgecrest and lightning struck a fire station but no injuries were reported. Numerous palm trees and power poles were knocked down, he said.

Southern California Edison reported power outages caused by lightning strikes stretching from Tulare County to San Bernardino County. The utility could not provide a tally of outages, but said Tulare County was most affected with some 4,100 customers without electricity.

The threat of thunderstorms is expected to diminish today.

The Associated Press contributed to this story.
