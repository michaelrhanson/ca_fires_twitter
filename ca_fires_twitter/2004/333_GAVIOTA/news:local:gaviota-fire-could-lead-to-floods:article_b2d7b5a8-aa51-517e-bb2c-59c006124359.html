Gaviota fire could lead to floods

  • Quintin Cushner/Staff Writer

As firefighters work to extinguish the Gaviota wildfire, other officials are working to prevent the floods, mudslides and rockslides that can occur when winter rains fall on the scorched terrain.

The 7,440-acre fire was fully contained by 831 firefighters Thursday, though there is no estimate yet of when it might be extinguished. A fire is declared contained when it is fully surrounded and there are no hot spots near its edges.

Since the blaze started shortly before noon Saturday, it has caused Highway 101 to close several times, most recently from 7 p.m. Tuesday until 2 a.m. Wednesday.

A home and three outbuildings have been burned, and hundreds of local residents were temporarily evacuated. Train service in the area remains halted as officials worked to repair a damaged railroad trestle, and local parks and beaches near the fire also are closed.

So far the cost of the fire is estimated at ,5,545,514, which will likely be shared between county, state and federal agencies, according to Capt. Charlie Johnson of the Santa Barbara County Fire Department.

The denuding of vegetation in areas scorched by the blaze makes the area more vulnerable to rain that could hit the area as early as October, said Tom Fayram, deputy public works director for Santa Barbara County.

The county will work alongside the U.S. Forest Service and the California Department of Transportation to determine what can be done to prepare for rushing water or large rocks heading from the fire area toward highways and populated areas.

In coming months, the county may speak with residents of Hollister Ranch and other homeowners nearby about devising a response plan if the rains do turn out to pose a problem, Fayram said.

Recommendations in the past have included advising residents to dig basins to impede mud and rock slides, and to place piles of dirt or sandbags to help deflect flood waters, he said.

Areas likely to experience flooding this season include the county/s Gaviota Creek Bridge, on the road into Hollister Ranch, was well as Gaviota State Beach, Fayram said.

If the rain does come, it may fill several creek beds near the Gaviota Tunnel and the San Onofre Canyon, according to Kevin Cooper, who runs the U.S. Forest Services/ Burned Area Emergency Rehab team.

Officials may set up debris catches 7 metal piping or fencing 7 in the creek beds to stop large objects from sliding into highways or residential areas, he said.

Such debris catches and rock netting likely will be set up near Highway 101 and several rural roads to help prevent rocks from falling on or near vehicles, according to Colin Jones of the California Department of Transportation, which oversees the structural safety of state highways.

The most imminent concern is the dry gravel slides, caused by loose, fine rocks set free by the rapid loss of vegetation, Cooper said.

Already, some of that rock has fallen on Highway 101 near the Gaviota Pass, Cooper said.

Also of concern are culverts that run under the highway, and whether they will be able to handle increased water, he said. Currently, Caltrans workers are clearing out those drainage routes to accommodate the increased flow of materials.

Officials also are considering re-seeding some of the areas lost to fire, Cooper said.

The California State Parks system, where about one-quarter of the burn occurred, avoids reseeding, instead depending on quick re-growth of native grasses, according to Virginia Gardner, ecologist with the California State Parks.

"If we have some good coastal fog, we should see some plants coming back in the next few months," Gardner said.

Luckily, the fire hit a region that is still a mostly safe distance from some of the larger housing developments on the South Coast, Cooper said.

Because of this, the wildfire will pose less of a problem than the much larger Painted Cave Fire, which burned hundreds of homes near Goleta in 1990, ravage entire neighborhoods, and later caused mudslides and flooding in residential areas, Cooper said.
