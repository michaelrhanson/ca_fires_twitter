Pattison Fire takes 13 West County homes

  • By Mike Taylor
  • Sep 7, 2004
  • Sep 7, 2004

High winds and tinder-dry conditions contributed to a firestorm that ignited Friday afternoon and sent thousands of west county residents fleeing to evacuation centers.

The Pattison Complex Fire started at 1:30 p.m. Friday on Pattison Road near Camanche Parkway South. Before nightfall, 3,000 residents were ordered to leave their homes as the fire roared toward the Quail Oaks, La Contenta and Rancho Calaveras subdivisions.

"This is a wind-driven fire," CDF Battalion Chief Kevin O'Meara said in the late afternoon Friday. He estimated winds were blowing at a steady 15 mph with gusts approaching 25 mph. "It's spotting from a quarter to a half mile ahead of the head of the fire."

The conflagration began when a 1974 Commander motorhome driven by James Batterton, 83, of Valley Springs, caught fire while he was driving the rig north on Camanche Parkway South.

The California Highway Patrol said the vehicle began to slow down and Batterton pulled over at a dirt shoulder and stopped. "Within minutes his motorhome was totally engulfed in flames," Officer Jim Clary said.

The winds pushed the flames south and to the east and very quickly threatened homes in the Burson area.

"The wind was hideous," said Sharon Torrence, public information officer for the Tuolumne/Calaveras Ranger Unit. "The fire just hop-scotched across the area."

Wayne Rush, who works the graveyard shift at the San Jose Mercury News, said he was asleep inside his Burson Road home when helicopters passing overhead awakened him.

"The front yard was on fire," Rush said. "I was worried about the roof," because fiery embers were landing there. Rush said there was ample clearance between the grasses on his property and the structures.

The flames crossed Highway 12 near Evans Road outside Burson and by 3:15 p.m., Calaveras County sheriff's deputies were driving up and down streets in the Rancho Calaveras subdivision ordering residents to leave.

Grover Inks of 2005 Highway 12 was protecting his house with a water hose when an air tanker, witnessing his efforts from above, decided to help out by dumping its load of sticky pink fire retardant on the house. The goo plastered his rooftop and the surrounding area, which included several vehicles.

Evacuation centers were set up at Valley Springs Elementary and Toyon Middle Schools, and some residents did use at least the elementary school shelter overnight. They were allowed back into their neighborhoods beginning Saturday morning.

Authorities had the Pacific Gas and Electric Co. switch off the power to the Rancho Calaveras and La Contenta subdivisions in mid-afternoon Friday.

"I was driving down Berkesey (Drive) and a spot blew up in front of me," said Sheriff Dennis Downum on Friday.

The firestorm traveled over a ridgeline and down into a densely populated portion of the Rancho Calaveras subdivision below Ross Drive.

Lee McNabb, who owns the Valley Springs Driving Range, was in Reno when the blaze broke out. His home was consumed by the flames.

"It took everything but the view," McNabb said Monday.

Lee and his wife, Joanie, had lived in the 3,000-square-foot home for about seven years.

"It was a nice place," Lee McNabb said. "I'm going to miss the deck, and steam room and sauna I built.

"Even all the stuff in the fire-proof safe burned."

The McNabbs are staying with friends. Lee said his insurance carrier is expected to visit him on Tuesday to begin the rebuilding process.

"I'll rebuild on the same place," McNabb said.

By midnight Friday, most of the evacuated residents were allowed to go back to their homes. A special CDF Fire Protection Command Team took control of the fire fighting operations at 6 a.m., Saturday.

Other homes near the McNabbs also were lost, but the fire was choosy, skipping some homes only to pounce on others.

Frank Diedrich, who lives nearby on Brooks Lane and Ross Drive, helped save his home by staying put and battling the blaze with a water hose and shovel. First, though, he ferried his six horses to safer pasture.

Another blaze broke out around 5:30 p.m. Friday that closed Highway 12. The Lime Creek incident became another branch of the Pattison incident. Torrence said 250 acres were consumed, and authorities believe there were problems with power lines in the area just north and east of Valley Springs, but the exact cause of the fire is still under investigation.

The CDF says 2,676 acres were charred during the Pattison Complex Fire. Thirteen homes were destroyed; two of which were under construction. Another residence sustained significant damage. Numerous outbuildings n 45 at last count n have been destroyed along with many vehicles and other equipment. Authorities are still examining the area to discover exactly what the fire destroyed.

At its peak, 1,400 firefighters on 118 engines, 40 hand crews, 16 bulldozers, six water tenders and numerous aircraft and helicopters battled the blaze. Authorities say the cost of fighting the fire has hit the $2 million mark, and that does not include damage estimates.

The fire also destroyed a 150,000-gallon Calaveras County Water District tank.

"The roof caught fire and then fell in to the tank," said Jeff Davidson, CCWD director from the area.

Bill Perley, assistant operations and maintenance superintendent, said a diver examined the tank Saturday and it has been isolated from the drinking water system. Drinking water lines in the affected area have been pressurized from other CCWD pumping stations in the area.

"The water is fine," Perley said. "There may be a little bit of sediment, but it's safe."

He added the lines should be flushed by CCWD personnel by the end of the week and a search has begun to find a temporary tank for the site.

CCWD officials went door-to-door Sunday telling residents on Ross Drive, Brooks Lane, and Leckie, Hagen and Didier roads to expect low flow rates and pressure until a temporary tank is installed. Perley asked that customers try to limit their use of water until the temporary tank is in place.

"Hopefully, that will be by the end of the week," Perley said Monday.

Calaveras County supervisors met for a special session Sunday and voted unanimously to declare the Pattison Fire's charred ground a local disaster area. Sheriff Downum told supervisors in a memorandum that public agency infrastructure losses from the fire are estimated at about $250,000 to $300,000, noting that all the losses have not yet been accounted for.

Torrence said the CDF incident team was expected to relinquish control of the Pattison Complex incident Monday evening.

"Residents can expect to see smoke within the burn," Torrence said. She suggested that residents who spot flare-ups call 911 to report any hot spots within the fire boundaries.

"It's easy to tell where good clearance saved homes," Torrence said. Good defensible space "is essential for us to protect structures."

Anyone who needs assistance in finding a place to stay, should call the Red Cross at (916) 997-5023.
