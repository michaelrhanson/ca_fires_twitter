Wildfire forces Boy Scout camp evacuation

By YVETTE URREA and JO MORELAND - Staff Writers
July 14, 2004 12 AM PT
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Show more sharing options
Share
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • LinkedIn
  • Email
  • Copy Link URLCopied!
  • Print

LAKE HENSHAW -- A wildfire which was started, officials said, bybottle rockets along Highway 79, roared toward the Mataguay BoyScout Camp on Tuesday afternoon, forcing the evacuation of 350scouts and parents.

Advertisement

As assistant Scoutmaster Nick Collura and about six scoutswaited to leave the camp in a sport utility vehicle, the sky wasdark with little visibility except for orange flames just 5 feetaway that were heating up the inside of the vehicle.

I didnt think we were going to get out of there, saidCollura, who is also a Los Angeles Police Officer, at WarnerSprings High School where evacuees gathered.

With the assistance of the San Diego County Sheriffs Departmentand California Department of Forestry & Fire Protectionfirefighters who set backfires around the camp, everyone wasevacuated safely.

Colluras group was among about 350 staff and scouts ranging inage from 6 to their early teens at Camp Mataguay, a 700-acre campabout 33 miles northeast of Escondido.

At 8 p.m. Tuesday, the Mataguay fire had burned 8,500 acres andwas 10 percent contained but officials had no estimated time forfull containment, CDF spokeswoman Roxanne Provaznik said.

The fire started as three separate fires that merged alongHighway 79. Fire investigators found remnants of bottle rocketswhere the fires began along the side of the road, CDF BattalionChief Mike Neil said.

We believe the person who started it had to know they weregoing to start a fire, Neil said.

The fire burned an undetermined number of outbuildings but nohomes had burned as of 8 p.m. Tuesday, CDF fire spokesman MattStreck said. Three firefighters suffered minor injuries, one ofthem had minor burns to his arm, CDF Division Chief Rick Hensonsaid.

He said they evacuated the camp and about 40 residences on StateRoute 22 in Ranchita where the fire was headed Tuesday evening.

The fire was initially spotted at 12:13 p.m. by a Border Patrolagent who first spotted one fire south of the Mataguay Campentrance on Highway 79 and was trying to put that out with a fireextinguisher when he suddenly saw another area on fire 500 yardsnorth of that on fire, Neil said. Later, they found a third areawhere the fire had also started beyond the second start area butall were within a mile of each other, he said.

Thyme Osborne, spokeswoman for Boy Scouts of America in SanDiego, said the Boy Scouts were told to evacuate the camp shortlybefore 1 p.m., she said.

We have an evacuation plan thats been there for years andyears and years, said Osborne.

Scouts, staff and parents were camping for the week at CampMataguay when the fires started, said Ron Schoenmehl, campdirector.

Jim Trageser, assistant online editor for The North CountyTimes, was camping with his son this week at the camp. As Trageserleft at 12:15 p.m. for his shift at the newspaper he said hespotted a Border Patrol agent trying to put out one of the fires aquarter mile from the Camp Mataguay entrance.

The blaze went from 20 feet wide to 50 feet within seconds,Trageser said.

Sheriffs deputies Steve McNamara assigned to the rural patroland Mark Andersen assigned to Valley Center took charge of theevacuations at the Boy Scout camp, sheriffs spokesman ChrisSaunders said.

They led one group of six or seven cars out and then the fireflashed over, so they waited and later let out a second group ofcars but then the fire flashed over again, Saunders said. Theydecided to put the rest of the group back in the dining hall andshelter in place, he said. After some time, they tried again butthe road was blocked by flames so they were lined up in an openfield with firefighters guarding them around it, Saunders said.

After about 3 1/2 hours, they got everyone out safely, hesaid.

Later parents greeted their sons with open arms when Boy ScoutTroop 669, evacuated from the camp, arrived shortly before 7 p.m.at a North County Times parking lot in Escondido.

It was, said 11-year-old Scout Alain Hungerford, the mostexciting camping trip he has ever been on.

We were at lunch, and then we saw a bunch of smoke, he said."Everybody was being calm. We had water and everything. We werefine.

Ten-year-old Adam Slutsky said he was a little nervous.

One of the guys said Stay calm. Everythings OK, said Adam."Then he comes back 15 minutes later and says Everybody run forthe cars. We werent calm any more.

With leaders urging them to jog, about 350 boys, leaders,parents and staff headed for cars and other vehicles.

As we got up to these cars, said Lloyd Keith of Escondido,camping with his nephew, they were just shoving kids in. Theydidnt care which car, just to get em away.

Troop Scoutmaster Charles Fleuret, 45, of Escondido said therewas time to get out and that was it. Escondido Troop 668 was alsoat the camp.

Parents praised the scout leaders for keeping the boys, some ofthem from as far away as Yuma, Ariz., calm and getting themout.

In Ramona, resident Phyllis Lynch was among those who wereconcerned when they first saw the dark plume of smoke billowing upon the northeastern horizon.

Just because the devastating Cedar fire burned through the arealast October and November doesnt mean there isnt plenty ofgreenery to burn through again, Lynch said.

The deadly fire that killed 14 people began as a small blaze andbecame a blow torch that caught firefighters, law enforcement andmany residents without warning.

Lynch said she feels more confident this time that themiscommunication that may have happened among authorities at thestart of last falls firestorm is less likely to happen now.

I think everybody will be aware that it needs to be handledimmediately, she said.
